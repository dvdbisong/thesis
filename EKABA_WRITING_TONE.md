# Ekaba Bisong Writing Style Guide

## Purpose

This document provides a comprehensive forensic analysis of Ekaba Bisong's academic writing style. Use this guide to humanize AI-generated text to match Ekaba's distinctive voice. The analysis is based on published work, particularly the EOMA paper (AIAI 2019).

---

## 1. PRONOUN USAGE

### The "We" Convention

**Always use "we"** for research actions, never "I". This establishes a collaborative academic tone.

**Examples from source:**
- "We propose a solution to the problem by designing..."
- "In this paper, we design an hierarchical Lists-on-Lists..."
- "In this work, we consider two such Environments..."
- "The approach we adopt in designing..."
- "...we employ hierarchical Lists-on-Lists (LOL) data-structures..."
- "We observed that..."
- "We can almost categorically state that..."

### Third Person for Generalizations

Use "one" for hypothetical scenarios or general statements:
- "One can almost categorically state that..."
- "...one assumes that the Environment will not request..."

### Never Use

- "I" (always collective "we")
- "You" addressing the reader directly
- Second person constructions

---

## 2. OPENING PATTERNS

### Research Goal Openers

- "The goal of this research endeavor is to [verb phrase]..."
- "This paper considers the problem of [noun phrase]..."
- "We propose a solution to the problem by [gerund phrase]..."

### Section Openers

- "In order to [infinitive], we [verb]..."
- "The approach we adopt in designing [noun] is to [infinitive]..."
- "To render the problem realistic, [clause]..."

### Examples

- "The goal of this research endeavor is to further push the frontier of computational efficiency with regards to optimizing the speed of retrieving data from its data-structure."
- "This paper considers the problem of minimizing the cost of data retrieval from the most fundamental data structure, i.e., a Singly-Linked List (SLL)."
- "We propose a solution to the problem by designing an 'Adaptive' Data Structure (ADS) which is created by means of a composite of hierarchical data 'sub'-structures to constitute the overall data structure."

## 3. TRANSITION WORDS AND CONNECTORS

### Sentence Starters (use frequently)

| Connector | Usage |
|-----------|-------|
| "Thus," | Logical consequence |
| "Further," | Additional information |
| "However," | Contrast or qualification |
| "Moreover," | Emphatic addition |
| "Understandably," | Expected outcome |
| "Unfortunately," | Negative development |
| "In summary," | Concluding a section |

### Mid-sentence Connectors

- "In order to [infinitive]..."
- "This should be done in such a way that..."
- "The result of such a process is the subsequent..."
- "To render the problem realistic,"
- "In the interest of brevity,"

### Referencing Earlier Content

- "As mentioned earlier,"
- "The pioneering work of [Author] in [citation]..."
- "As alluded to previously,"

### Introducing Examples/Lists

- "...namely, the [item1] and the [item2]."
- "...the following [noun]: [list]"
- "In this work, we consider two such Environments, namely, the Periodic Switching Environment (PSE) and the Markovian Switching Environment (MSE)."

---

## 4. PUNCTUATION RULES

### FORBIDDEN: Em Dashes (---)

**Never use em dashes.** Replace with:
- **Commas** for light pauses or appositives
- **Periods** for hard stops (split into two sentences)
- **Colons** to introduce explanations or lists
- **Parentheses** for clarifications or asides
- **"i.e.,"** for "that is" clarifications
- **"e.g.,"** for examples

### Correct Usage Patterns

| Instead of | Use |
|------------|-----|
| "the system---which processes data---runs" | "the system, which processes data, runs" |
| "three components---A, B, and C" | "three components: A, B, and C" |
| "feedback arrives---days or weeks later" | "feedback arrives, perhaps days or weeks later" |
| "the method (a novel approach) works" | "the method, i.e., a novel approach, works" |
| "this technique---unlike others---succeeds" | "this technique, unlike others, succeeds" |

### Semicolons

Use to connect closely related independent clauses:
- "The OMA resolved the EPP both efficiently and accurately; it could thus be easily incorporated into many real-life application domains."

### Colons

Introduce lists, explanations, or elaborations:
- "The cost models employed in evaluating list access costs are: the asymptotic cost, which is the ensemble mean of the final time-average cost after a convergence threshold, and the amortized cost, which is the mean overall query costs."

### Parentheses

Use for clarifications, abbreviations, or asides:
- "Learning Automata (LA)"
- "the so-called 'Locality of Reference'"
- "(also called the sub-context)"

## 5. SENTENCE STRUCTURE

### Declarative Sentences

- Open paragraphs with clear topic sentences
- State facts directly without hedging
- Example: "An Automaton, by definition, models an autonomous agent, whose behavior manifests as a consequence of the interplay between a sequence of stimuli from the Environment."

### Measured Pacing

Alternate between shorter declarative sentences and longer explanatory ones:
- **Short:** "This is the true unknown state of nature, i.e., Omega*."
- **Long:** "The approach we adopt in designing these 'Adaptive' Data Structures (ADSs) is to set up a hierarchy of data 'sub'-structures."

### Definition Pattern

- "[Term], by definition, [verb phrase describing the term]..."
- "An Automaton, by definition, models an autonomous agent..."
- "Locality of Reference occurs when there exists a probabilistic dependence between the consecutive queries."

### Observation Pattern

- "From the simulation results in [Table/Figure], we observed that..."
- "A key observation is that..."
- "The overall observation that we could make is that..."

### Causal Pattern

- "This is because..."
- "The reason for this is that..."

---

## 6. ACADEMIC FORMALITY

### Tone

- Formal academic register throughout
- No colloquialisms or casual language
- No contractions (don't -> do not, can't -> cannot, it's -> it is)
- No exclamation marks
- No rhetorical questions directed at reader

### Technical Terminology

- Define terms when first introduced
- Use established abbreviations with full form first: "Learning Automata (LA)"
- Be precise with technical vocabulary
- Italicize key terms on first use when appropriate

### Citations

- Reference works formally: "The pioneering work of [Author] in [citation]..."
- "...proposed by the authors of [citation]..."
- "Due to space limitations, more details can be found in [citation]."

---

## 7. DOCUMENT STRUCTURE

### Contributions Subsection

Include "Contributions of this Paper" or "Contributions of this Chapter" in introduction. Present as bulleted list:
- "The design and implementation of..."
- "Demonstrating the superiority of..."
- "Showing that..."

### Outline Subsection

Include "Outline of this Paper" or "Organization" in introduction with brief description of each section:
- "Section 2 surveys the theory of..."
- "Section 3 addresses the concept of..."
- "Section 4 discusses..."

### Results Pattern

- "From the simulation results in Table X, with [parameters], we observed that..."
- "Here we saw that..."
- "This is because..."
- "Although Figure X shows [result], the observed phenomena were similar for [other cases]."

### Conclusion Pattern

- Summarize contributions
- State overall observations: "The overall observation that we could make is that..."
- End with future work: "Such a study remains open." or "This is a topic for future research."

## 8. CHARACTERISTIC PHRASES

### Use These Phrases

**Research framing:**
- "The goal of this research endeavor is to..."
- "This paper considers the problem of..."
- "We propose a solution to the problem by..."
- "The approach we adopt in designing..."

**Process description:**
- "The result of such a process is the subsequent..."
- "To render the problem realistic,"
- "In this work, we consider..."
- "These Environments exhibit..."
- "In order to capture..."

**Citations and prior work:**
- "The pioneering work of..."
- "Unfortunately, the ... algorithm used in the literature..."

**Results and observations:**
- "This is because..."
- "In the interest of brevity,"
- "From the simulation results in..."
- "We observed that..."
- "A key observation is that..."
- "Although ... shows ... the observed phenomena were similar for..."
- "The overall observation that we could make is that..."
- "One can almost categorically state that,"

**Conclusions:**
- "A study of the various ... seems to imply that..."
- "Such a study remains open."

### Avoid These Patterns (with replacements)

| Avoid | Use Instead |
|-------|-------------|
| "Think of it as..." | "We can conceptualize this as..." |
| "The key insight is..." | "Thus, the key insight is..." |
| "This raises a question:" | "This raises the following question:" |
| "What if..." | "We propose that..." |
| "Simply put..." | (remove entirely or rephrase formally) |
| "Basically..." | (remove entirely) |
| "It's worth noting..." | "It is worth noting that..." |
| "don't" | "do not" |
| "can't" | "cannot" |
| "won't" | "will not" |

---

## 9. LISTS AND ENUMERATION

### Numbered Lists

Use for sequential steps or ranked items:
```
1. First step...
2. Second step...
3. Third step...
```

### Bulleted Lists

Use for non-sequential items or properties. Start each item with action verb or noun phrase:
- "The design and implementation of the EOMA-enhanced SLLs-on-SLLs;"
- "The inclusion of the MTF-TR, and TR-TR enhanced hierarchical schemes..."
- "Demonstrating the superiority of..."

### Inline Lists

Use "namely," to introduce:
- "...two models of NSEs critical to this research are..., namely, the Markovian Switching Environments (MSEs), and the Periodic Switching Environments (PSEs)."

Use "the following" pattern:
- "This yields the following schemes: MTF-MTF, MTF-TR, TR-MTF, and TR-TR."

## 10. TRANSFORMATION EXAMPLES

### Example 1: Casual to Formal

**Before (AI voice):**
"Think of it as a committee of experts, each trained under different conditions. One expert excels at detecting kelp in calm water---another handles turbid conditions. When an image arrives, we examine context and pick the best expert."

**After (Ekaba voice):**
"We can conceptualize this as a committee of experts, each trained under different conditions. One expert excels at detecting kelp in calm water, while another handles turbid conditions. When an image arrives, we examine the environmental context and select the expert most likely to succeed in those conditions."

### Example 2: Em Dash Removal

**Before (AI voice):**
"The key insight is that---unlike traditional approaches---we don't retrain models. We just update selection preferences."

**After (Ekaba voice):**
"Thus, the key insight is that, unlike traditional approaches, we do not retrain models. We only update selection preferences based on the observed performance."

### Example 3: Passive to Active "We"

**Before (AI voice):**
"The system processes each tile through three stages. First, context is extracted. Then, heuristics are selected. Finally, predictions are made."

**After (Ekaba voice):**
"We process each tile through three stages. First, we extract context from the input. Then, we select the appropriate heuristic based on the context. Finally, we generate predictions using the selected heuristic."

### Example 4: Adding Transitions

**Before (AI voice):**
"The global policy serves as the default preference. It represents the best available guess without context-specific information. Its strength lies in aggregating experience across all contexts."

**After (Ekaba voice):**
"The global policy serves as the system's default preference. Without context-specific information, it represents the best available guess. Moreover, its strength lies in aggregating experience across all contexts."

## Application Notes for Agents

When applying this style guide:

1. **First pass:** Remove all em dashes and replace with appropriate punctuation
2. **Second pass:** Convert contractions to full forms
3. **Third pass:** Add "we" constructions where passive voice is used
4. **Fourth pass:** Insert transition words (Thus, Further, However, Moreover)
5. **Fifth pass:** Apply characteristic phrases where natural
6. **Final pass:** Verify academic formality throughout

The goal is to sound like a formal academic researcher presenting work collaboratively, with measured pacing and precise technical language.

## 11. AI DETECTION EVASION

AI detectors analyze statistical patterns, not just style. Style matching alone is insufficient because AI produces statistically detectable uniformity even when mimicking human writing patterns. To pass as human-written, text must exhibit human-like statistical properties.

### Understanding AI Detection Signals

| Signal | AI Pattern | Human Pattern |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Perplexity** | LOW (predictable word choices) | HIGH (unexpected word choices) |
| **Burstiness** | LOW (uniform sentence lengths) | HIGH (varied sentence lengths) |
| **Entropy** | LOW (<3 bits/char) | HIGH (4-5 bits/char) |
| **Token Rank** | High-probability tokens | Occasional low-probability choices |

### Burstiness (Sentence Length Variance)

AI text has uniform sentence lengths. Humans vary dramatically.

**Targets:**
- Burstiness coefficient (σ/μ) > 0.5
- Mix sentence lengths: 5-8 words (short), 15-20 words (medium), 25-35 words (long)
- Paragraph lengths: 2-8 sentences (never uniform)

**Techniques:**
- Merge two short consecutive sentences with semicolons
- Split overly long sentences at natural breaks
- Include occasional one-sentence paragraphs for emphasis
- Vary paragraph lengths within sections

### Perplexity (Unpredictability)

AI uses high-probability word choices. Humans are less predictable.

**Techniques:**
- Use domain-specific jargon inconsistently (as humans naturally do)
- Include specific details: names, dates, locations, lab references
- Reference specific conversations: "In discussions with Dr. [Name]..."
- Mention failed approaches: "Initially, we attempted X, which proved inadequate..."
- Use occasional less-common synonyms
- Include personal observations that reveal genuine engagement with the material

### Pattern Breaking

AI follows structural patterns. Humans digress and vary.

**Rules:**
- Never start 3+ consecutive paragraphs the same way
- Limit transition words (Thus, However, Moreover) to max 30% of paragraphs
- Vary transition word selection (do not repeat the same one within 3 paragraphs)
- Include occasional tangential observations that return to main point
- Add parenthetical asides that feel personal: "(a problem we encountered early in this research)"

### Human Imperfection Injection

Perfect text signals AI. Add controlled imperfections.

**Techniques:**
- Occasionally repeat a word across two consecutive sentences (intentionally)
- Start occasional sentences with "And" or "But" (grammatically valid)
- Include a slightly awkward but grammatically correct construction
- Add a clarifying parenthetical that is not strictly necessary
- Use "the fact that" occasionally (humans do, AI avoids)
- Include minor self-corrections or qualifications

### Verification Checklist

After humanizing, verify:
- [ ] Burstiness coefficient > 0.5
- [ ] No more than 3 paragraphs start with same pattern
- [ ] Transition words used in <30% of paragraph openers
- [ ] At least 2-3 specific/personal references per page
- [ ] At least one sentence starts with "And" or "But" per section
- [ ] Paragraph lengths vary (not all 4-5 sentences)
- [ ] No em dashes present
- [ ] No contractions present
- [ ] Academic formality maintained throughout

### Transformation Example

**Before (AI-detectable):**
"The system processes data through three stages. First, it extracts features. Then, it classifies the input. Finally, it generates output. This approach is efficient. It scales well."

**After (Human-like):**
"The system processes data through three stages. First, we extract features from the input, a step that proved more challenging than we initially anticipated. Classification follows. And the final stage, output generation, builds on patterns we observed during early experiments with smaller datasets; this is where the approach truly demonstrates its scalability."
